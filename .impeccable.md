## Design Context

### Users
Three audiences in priority order:
1. **Low-income renters in housing crisis** — stressed, possibly on limited hardware, unfamiliar with AMI/LIHTC jargon, need clarity and confidence fast
2. **Social workers / case managers** — professionals navigating the app on behalf of clients; value speed, density, and export capabilities
3. **Congressional App Challenge judges** — evaluating impact and technical polish; want a showcase of civic tech done right

### Brand Personality
**Calm, trustworthy, competent.** Like a government resource that actually works.
Three words: **clear · reliable · civic**

The app deals with a serious problem (housing insecurity). It should feel like a tool people can trust with important decisions — not a startup product trying to look slick, not a charity site dripping with sentimentality.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Dark theme** — users may be searching late at night, on older monitors, in stressful moments. Dark reduces cognitive load.
- **Muted green accent** — existing `oklch(60% 0.18 145)` works: earthy, trustworthy, not corporate-blue, not neon
- **Typography**: System font stack is acceptable for density/legibility but could be elevated. Avoid editorial display fonts — this is a functional tool, not a magazine.
- **Anti-references**:
  - Real estate apps (Zillow/Redfin): photo-heavy, agent-focused, commercial → opposite vibe
  - Generic SaaS dashboards: card grid monoculture, teal everywhere, Notion-style padding → too sterile

### Design Principles
1. **Clarity over cleverness** — every element should immediately communicate its purpose; no decorative chrome
2. **Density with breathing room** — show as much useful information as possible without overwhelming; trust whitespace
3. **Legibility under stress** — users may be anxious; high-contrast text, clear hierarchy, no ambiguous affordances
4. **Civic dignity** — feels like a well-run public library, not a startup or a charity; competent and matter-of-fact
5. **Trust signals** — data sources, AMI explainers, and accuracy cues should be visible without dominating
